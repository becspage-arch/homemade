# Playbook

The actionable output. What Homemade posts, on which platforms, in what order, in what voice.

This playbook covers only the bootstrap phase — Rebecca and Claude, no UGC, no creator programme, no paid promotion, no on-camera filming. The cooking category leads. Other categories come online as the tutorial library covers them.

## Lead-platform recommendation

**Pinterest is the platform Homemade leads with.**

The reasoning:

1. **Lowest production cost.** Pins are still photographs (or illustrations) with text overlay. No video required to begin.
2. **Highest evergreen leverage.** A pin published in month one earns impressions in month thirty-six. Every other platform punishes old content.
3. **Audience demographic alignment.** Pinterest's median user is the closest match to Homemade's stated audience (women, 30–55, slow-living, planning-led) of any platform in the research set.
4. **Fully public, no anti-bot risk.** Pinterest can be researched and operated without the platform-policing concerns that apply to Instagram or TikTok.
5. **The publishing pipeline already produces the source material.** Each tutorial published on homemade.education yields 5–10 pins automatically. The marginal cost of a pin is trivial.
6. **Pinterest is brand-channel-friendly.** Personality matters less here than on any other platform. Food52, BBC Good Food, NYT Cooking and King Arthur Baking all demonstrate the model at scale.

**YouTube long-form is the second-priority platform** because it pairs neatly with Pinterest: a single long-form video produces YouTube Shorts plus Reels plus TikToks plus the source material for the pin set. But YouTube needs video production capacity that Pinterest doesn't.

**TikTok and Instagram Reels are downstream of YouTube.** Once the long-form video pipeline is producing one video per week, the same source material feeds three short-form surfaces.

**Substack is the direct-audience-relationship play.** Start small from day one (weekly letter that mirrors one published tutorial) and let it compound through cross-recommendations.

**Instagram static and stories are deprioritised** but the brand-channel account exists from day one as a name reservation and as the Reels surface.

## Order of operations

Phase one of social: Pinterest only. The day the splash gate drops, the first 30–50 pins ship from the tutorials already published.

Phase two: weekly Substack letter, mirroring one tutorial per week, starts in parallel.

Phase three: the first YouTube long-form is produced (one anchor tutorial filmed in the brand-direction kitchen set-up). The Shorts, TikToks, and Reels are cut from it.

Phase four: full cross-platform cadence — one weekly YouTube long-form, two to four short-form cuts per week on TikTok / Reels / Shorts, one weekly Substack letter, ongoing Pinterest publication.

The phasing is not time-bound; it's capacity-bound. Each phase begins when the previous phase is sustainable, not on a calendar.

## First-90-days post-calendar template

The 90-day window assumes phases one and two are running and phase three is starting around the middle. Weeks 1–4 are Pinterest + Substack only. Weeks 5–8 add YouTube long-form. Weeks 9–13 add the short-form cuts.

This is a template, not a script. The volume and pacing are designed for sustainability with the two-person team.

### Weeks 1–4: Pinterest and Substack only

**Pinterest cadence:** 5–10 pins per day, drawn from the existing tutorial library. Generated programmatically from the tutorial's hero illustration plus key step illustrations.

**Substack cadence:** one letter per week, Sunday morning. 600–1,200 words. Mirrors a single published tutorial — the editorial framing of why this tutorial, plus the link, plus a short secondary note.

**Themes per week:**

- Week 1 — Sourdough basics. Pinterest pins from the sourdough anchor tutorial; Substack letter on first sourdough.
- Week 2 — Seasonal cooking (whatever is in season in the British calendar). Pinterest pins from any current seasonal tutorial; Substack letter on the season.
- Week 3 — Preserves (jam, jelly, chutney appropriate to season). Pinterest pin set; Substack letter on preserving the harvest.
- Week 4 — A second cooking technique (stocks, sauces, or pastry, depending on which tutorial is ready). Pin set; Substack letter introducing the technique.

### Weeks 5–8: add YouTube long-form

**Pinterest cadence:** continues at 5–10 pins per day.

**Substack cadence:** continues weekly. Letters now mirror the YouTube release where possible.

**YouTube cadence:** one long-form per week, Saturday morning. 8–15 minutes. Calm overhead, ambient sound, generic narration or no narration. Recipe drawn from a current tutorial.

**Themes per week:**

- Week 5 — Sourdough anchor video. Releases alongside the existing tutorial.
- Week 6 — Sourdough scoring patterns (short-form-friendly content, runs longer to support YouTube algorithm).
- Week 7 — A laminated-pastry video (croissant or puff pastry). Visually rich, ASMR-friendly.
- Week 8 — A British-season recipe (whatever is currently in season; could be a summer salad, an autumn preserve, a winter stew).

### Weeks 9–13: add short-form cuts

**Pinterest cadence:** continues at 5–10 pins per day.

**Substack cadence:** continues weekly.

**YouTube long-form:** continues weekly.

**Short-form (TikTok + Reels + Shorts):** 2–4 posts per week per platform. Each post is a 30–60 second cut from the week's long-form video. Same cuts across all three platforms with platform-specific captions.

**Themes per week (covering both long-form and short-form):**

- Week 9 — Bread shaping deep dive. Short-form cuts focus on the single shaping moment.
- Week 10 — Tomato season (the actual British timing may shift the order; align to the calendar). Garden-side content begins on YouTube alongside the kitchen content.
- Week 11 — A choux-pastry tutorial. ASMR-friendly piping shots cut into short-form.
- Week 12 — A herb-growing tutorial. Time-lapse footage cut into short-form.
- Week 13 — A holiday or season-anchor recipe appropriate to current calendar timing.

## Reusable content templates

Five templates Homemade returns to across the year. Each one specifies the format, the hook, the structure, and the brand voice constraints.

### Template 1 — The 60-second overhead recipe demo

**Platforms:** TikTok, Reels, Shorts.

**Hook:** the finished result, framed in the brand-direction styling (linen napkin, ceramic plate, soft daylight). Held for 0.5–1 second, then cut to the start of the recipe.

**Structure:**

- 0–3 seconds: finished result.
- 3–15 seconds: ingredients laid out, one by one, with serif-overlay names.
- 15–45 seconds: the cook itself. Overhead, hands-only, no voice. Ambient sound carries the rhythm.
- 45–55 seconds: the finished result again, plated, held longer.
- 55–60 seconds: a single serif card with the tutorial title and "Full recipe on homemade.education."

**Music:** none, or a quiet instrumental from the TikTok Commercial Music Library if ambient sound is too sparse.

**Caption pattern (TikTok):**

> Sourdough that proves overnight in the fridge.
> 75% hydration, fermented at 4°C, baked from cold.
> Full tutorial on homemade.education
>
> #sourdough #slowbaking #homemadeeducation

**Caption pattern (Reels):** identical but with 1–2 sentences of additional context.

**Voice constraint:** no banned phrases, British English, metric-first.

### Template 2 — The 90-second hands-only craft tutorial

**Platforms:** TikTok, Reels, Shorts.

**Hook:** the finished craft piece (loaf scored to a particular pattern, granny square in a finished colour combination, garden bed at harvest).

**Structure:**

- 0–3 seconds: finished piece.
- 3–10 seconds: materials laid out with serif overlays.
- 10–80 seconds: hands-only demonstration of the technique, with on-screen step counter (1/4, 2/4, etc.) in serif type.
- 80–90 seconds: the finished piece again, plus tutorial link card.

**Music:** ambient or quiet instrumental.

**Caption pattern:** identical to template 1 with technique-specific keyword.

### Template 3 — The Pinterest pin-burst from a published tutorial

**Platforms:** Pinterest.

**Per tutorial, generate:**

1. **Hero pin.** Tutorial title in display serif over the hero illustration. Vertical 1000 × 1500.
2. **Supplies pin.** Illustrated layout of the required supplies, with the title "What you'll need for [tutorial name]."
3. **Step pin × 2–4.** Each step pin shows the illustration for that step with its instruction caption.
4. **Finished-result pin.** Single hero shot of the finished outcome (illustration or photograph), title overlay.
5. **Glossary or chart pin (where applicable).** A diagram or chart (companion planting table, sourdough timeline, stitch dictionary entry, sowing calendar) with the tutorial title.

**Pin description pattern:**

> [Recipe or technique name]. [Specific detail — e.g. "75% hydration sourdough fermented overnight in the fridge." or "Three companion plants for tomatoes in a UK kitchen garden."] Full tutorial via the link.

**Cadence:** publish pins on a slow drip rather than all at once. Five to ten pins per day, drawn from the back-catalogue.

### Template 4 — The 10-minute YouTube anchor video

**Platforms:** YouTube long-form.

**Hook (first 15 seconds):** finished result + one-sentence context (either narrated or as a serif overlay) + cut to ingredients.

**Structure:**

- 0:00–0:15 — hook.
- 0:15–1:00 — ingredients with serif overlays, one by one.
- 1:00–7:30 — the process itself. Overhead, occasional close-ups, ambient sound, optional generic narration on the more complex steps.
- 7:30–9:00 — the finished result, plated and held.
- 9:00–9:30 — a serif card summarising the recipe (measurements + key timings) + tutorial link.
- 9:30–10:00 — slow fade on the finished dish, no outro music, no spoken sign-off.

**Title pattern:** "[Recipe name]: [specific detail]." E.g. "Sourdough That Proves in the Fridge: 75% Hydration, Overnight."

**Description pattern:** two-sentence summary, ingredients list (metric first), chapter timestamps, link to homemade.education tutorial, source attribution if a public-domain recipe was adapted.

**Chapters:** every section break is a chapter timestamp. This materially improves watch-time and ranking.

### Template 5 — The weekly Substack letter

**Platforms:** Substack.

**Structure:**

- 50–80 word lede. A concrete observation, ingredient, or instruction. No scene-setting.
- 200–400 word framing. The editorial reason this tutorial is being highlighted this week (season, technique, response to something in the news, anniversary of a tutorial drop).
- 100–200 word secondary note. A related smaller piece — a substitution worth knowing, a relevant glossary term, a public-domain source the tutorial drew from.
- A direct link to the tutorial, formatted as the final paragraph.

**Subject-line pattern:** "[Recipe or technique name]" or "[Season] this week" — short, concrete, no clickbait.

**Voice constraint:** the brand-voice rules apply more strictly here than anywhere else. The audience reads the letter carefully.

**No wrap-up sentence.** No "Happy baking," no "Until next week." The last paragraph is the link.

## Production stack

Tool subscriptions are fine; paid humans are not (per `feedback_no_hiring_yet.md`).

**Camera:** a recent iPhone or Pixel suffices for everything. A second device for time-lapse is helpful but not required.

**Tripod and overhead rig:** the cheapest "overhead phone arm" desk mount on Amazon. £20–£40.

**Microphone:** a clip-on lavalier or a small directional mic with a 3.5mm or Lightning / USB-C connector. £20–£60. The single highest-ROI production purchase.

**Lighting:** soft daylight from a window. A reflector card (a white board or a sheet of cartridge paper) bounces light back into shadow. Avoid lamps unless they're warm-temperature LEDs.

**Editing — video:**

- **CapCut** (free, web + mobile) for short-form. Templates, captions, music library. Good enough for everything up to YouTube long-form.
- **DaVinci Resolve** (free) for YouTube long-form if more control is needed. Steeper learning curve.

**Editing — Pinterest pins and Instagram carousels:**

- **Canva** (free tier sufficient at start, paid Pro tier later) with a saved Homemade template — the typography, palette, and pin dimensions locked.
- **Figma** as an alternative for more design control.

**Illustration generation:**

- **Flux 1.1 Pro Ultra** or **Midjourney with `--sref`** or **Stable Diffusion XL with a style LoRA** (the candidates flagged in the content pipeline plan). Style locked once the test results land. Used for tutorial hero illustrations, step illustrations, and Pinterest pin assets.

**Music:**

- **TikTok Commercial Music Library** for TikTok and Reels.
- **YouTube Audio Library** for YouTube.
- **Epidemic Sound** or **Artlist** (paid subscription) if music quality becomes a bottleneck.

**Scheduling / publishing:**

- **Buffer** or **Later** (free tiers) for Pinterest scheduling at volume. Pinterest's own Pin Scheduler is also usable but more limited.
- **Substack's native scheduler** for the weekly letter.

**Analytics:**

- **YouTube Studio**, **Pinterest Analytics**, **Substack stats** — native platform analytics are sufficient at this stage.
- **PostHog** (already in the Homemade stack) for cross-platform UTM-tagged traffic to homemade.education.

Total recurring cost: under £100 / month at most aggressive setup, can launch at £0 / month using free tiers only.

## Voice translation — from page to screen

The Homemade written voice (Alice Waters / Monty Don / Erin Boyle / Nigel Slater / Vita Sackville-West register, with the anti-AI rules) translates onto screen as serif overlay text — Fraunces for the display lines, Lora for any body. The on-screen text is the voice; the moving image is the demonstration.

### Worked example 1 — sourdough TikTok overlay

**Source paragraph from the tutorial:**

> A loaf to make on a Saturday, when there is time to wait. The long fermentation does most of the work; you only need to remember to come back to it.

**On-screen text across a 60-second overhead Reel:**

- 0–3 seconds (over the finished loaf): "Sourdough that proves overnight in the fridge."
- 8–11 seconds (over the flour and water): "75% hydration. Type 65 flour."
- 25–28 seconds (over the shaped loaf): "12 hours in the fridge at 4°C."
- 50–53 seconds (over the baked loaf): "Bakes from cold."
- 55–60 seconds (over the slice): "Full tutorial on homemade.education."

**What didn't translate:** the "Saturday" framing, the "remember to come back to it" warmth. That belongs in the tutorial page and the Substack letter. The Reel is the demonstration; the page is the story.

### Worked example 2 — tomato-sowing Reel overlay

**Source paragraph:**

> Tomatoes are forgiving plants, but they reward attention. A tomato grown well, staked early, watered deeply, fed when it asks, tastes like nothing you can buy.

**On-screen text across a 90-second Reel:**

- 0–3 seconds (over a finished tomato in a hand): "Tomatoes that taste of summer."
- 10–13 seconds (over the seed packet): "Sow indoors from March."
- 30–33 seconds (over the seedling at first true leaves): "Pot on at first true leaves."
- 55–58 seconds (over the staked outdoor plant): "Stake when planting out."
- 75–78 seconds (over the watering can): "Water deeply, feed weekly."
- 85–90 seconds (over the harvested tomato): "Full tutorial on homemade.education."

The "tastes like nothing you can buy" line stays on the page. The Reel demonstrates the technique; the tutorial carries the voice.

### Worked example 3 — Substack lede

**Source approach (the wrong way — generic AI-fronted lede). This is a labelled negative example; the banned phrases below appear intentionally to demonstrate the AI register:**

> In the world of slow living, sourdough stands as a testament to the rewarding nature of homemade baking. Today, let's dive into this timeless tradition.

**Source approach (the Homemade voice):**

> Saturday is the sourdough day, when there is time to wait. The long fermentation does most of the work. You only need to come back to the dough.
>
> This week's tutorial is the loaf we kept coming back to during the testing. 75% hydration, Type 65 flour, fermented in the fridge overnight, baked from cold.
>
> Full tutorial on homemade.education →

The brand-voice rules are observed throughout. No banned phrases, no banned openers, no wrap-up sentence, British English, concrete specifics, varied sentence length, no negation pattern.

## Cross-posting strategy

**The flow that produces the most output for the least input:**

1. **A single YouTube long-form video is produced.** This is the most-effort piece of content.
2. **2–4 short-form cuts** of the long-form are produced. Each cut is 30–60 seconds and focuses on a single moment (the dough slap, the score, the harvest, the finished plating).
3. **The same short-form cuts post to TikTok, Reels, and Shorts.** Captions vary per platform; the video itself is the same.
4. **The tutorial published on homemade.education** produces 5–10 Pinterest pins automatically via the publishing pipeline.
5. **A Substack letter** is written referencing the tutorial and the long-form video.
6. **An Instagram carousel** is built from the tutorial's TipTap structure — 8–10 illustrated frames covering the technique. This is platform-original content but uses assets already in the tutorial library.

The asymmetry between input and output is the structural advantage. One published tutorial produces approximately 15–25 individual social posts across all platforms once the pipeline is running.

## Growth milestones and what changes at each

The milestones below are written for the lead platform (Pinterest first, then YouTube once the long-form pipeline is live).

### 1,000 Pinterest followers / 1,000 YouTube subscribers

Early indicator that the format is landing. No process changes. Continue the established cadence. Begin tracking which pins / videos earn the highest saves and watch-time — those signals shape the next batch of content.

### 10,000 followers on the lead platform

Audience is established. Begin cross-recommendations on Substack (reach out to adjacent slow-living newsletters with offer of mutual recommendations). Consider opening Instagram and TikTok cadence to 3–5 posts per week from the established 2–3.

At this scale, audience feedback becomes a meaningful signal. Read comments and saves carefully. The audience will tell Homemade what to publish next.

### 100,000 followers on the lead platform

This is the scale where the brand can deliberately introduce a personality element if it serves the strategy — a named editor's signature on the Substack letter, a recurring narrator on YouTube, a specific designer-teacher for one of the categories. The strict "no personality" stance was a launch-phase constraint, not a permanent rule.

This is also the scale where paid partnerships become possible without distorting the brand voice — a yarn supplier sponsoring a knitting series, a flour producer sponsoring a sourdough series. (Per `feedback_no_hiring_yet.md`, paid creator partnerships remain off the table; this is brand-to-brand sponsorship at the editorial level, which is a different relationship.)

At 100K the publication has enough audience to support a Phase 6 creator programme — unpaid designer-teachers contributing content under the editorial standard. This is the explicit handoff point.

### Above 100,000

Beyond this point, growth depends on category expansion (adding gardening at depth after cooking is established, then crochet, then knitting) and on the creator programme adding voices the brand channel cannot produce alone.

## What we explicitly don't do at this stage

- **No filming Rebecca.** Hands and worktop only.
- **No voice with personality.** Generic narration when narration is used, or no narration.
- **No paid creator collaborations.** Per `feedback_no_hiring_yet.md`.
- **No paid advertising.** Organic only.
- **No purchased followers, growth services, engagement pods.** All zero-value, all brand-damaging.
- **No clickbait hooks.** "You won't believe," "Wait for it," "Stop scrolling" are off the table.
- **No emoji-heavy captions.** Restrained brand voice on every platform.
- **No engagement begging.** "Comment 'YES' if you love sourdough" is the opposite of Homemade.
- **No personality manufacturing.** If the brand needs a face, that's a deliberate Phase 6 decision, not a workaround for slow growth.
- **No reposting other creators' content without explicit rights.** Even the curated / aggregator angle requires clean rights chains.

## Pinterest-first action list

The concrete first actions, in order, the day the splash gate drops:

1. Create or claim the Homemade Pinterest brand account. `pinterest.com/homemadeeducation`.
2. Verify homemade.education in Pinterest Business settings (this enables analytics and Rich Pins).
3. Enable Rich Pins on the tutorial pages (open-graph metadata + Pinterest validator). This makes the tutorial title and description appear under every pin automatically.
4. Build the Pinterest pin template in Canva or Figma — Fraunces title overlay, sage on cream, brand-direction palette. Save as a reusable template.
5. Create the initial board set (Cooking, Baking, Gardening, Crochet, Knitting) even if some are empty at launch. Add a board description per board referencing the relevant Homemade tutorials.
6. Generate the first 30–50 pins from the existing tutorial library and schedule across the first three weeks.
7. Set up a UTM convention so traffic from Pinterest pins arrives at homemade.education with the right attribution (`?utm_source=pinterest&utm_medium=pin&utm_campaign=[tutorial-slug]`).

The Substack and YouTube actions begin in parallel but Pinterest is the first one.
