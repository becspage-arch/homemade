# NYT Cooking

- **Handle:** @nytcooking
- **Primary platform:** Multi-platform (Pinterest, Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, app, web)
- **Cross-platform:** Centred on cooking.nytimes.com
- **Niche / sub-niche:** Cooking / brand-channel publisher

## Scale and trajectory

- Instagram: ~2.8M followers as of 2021 reporting — likely materially larger now.
- TikTok: hashtag #nytcooking has 20.3M+ tagged posts; the account itself runs at scale.
- Pinterest: brand-channel account established 2014.
- A premium-publisher reference for what a brand-channel cooking presence looks like at the top of the market.

## Format

Multi-format brand-channel. Instagram Reels of overhead recipe demos, photographer-styled finished-dish shots, occasional pieces from named NYT food writers but the channel identity is the brand not the personality. TikTok mirrors the Reels strategy. Pinterest pins are recipe-led, photo-and-title format, link to the recipe page (which sits behind the NYT Cooking paywall).

The on-screen captions in Reels and TikToks are quiet — short, declarative, no exclamation, no excessive emoji.

## Engagement signals

Hashtag volume on TikTok (20.3M+ posts) is the most striking signal: NYT Cooking has become a category descriptor. Audience treats the brand as the trust signal regardless of the specific writer or recipe origin.

## Standout patterns

- The recipe announcement: a 30-second Reel showing the finished dish, then a rapid-cut process, then "Recipe in our app."
- The technique short: 45 seconds explaining a single technique (how to dice an onion, how to fold meringue). Brand-format, no recurring presenter.
- Pinterest pin-bursts on seasonal moments — Thanksgiving baking, summer salads, Christmas roasts.

## What Homemade can take

- **A publisher's brand can carry social.** NYT Cooking did not need a face — it inherited the New York Times reputation. Homemade is building that reputation from zero, but the model (publisher-led brand-channel content) is the structural analogue.
- **Multi-platform with consistent brand voice.** The same recipe reads as NYT Cooking on a Reel, a Pinterest pin, and the app. Homemade's locked palette and typography enable the same coherence.
- **Quiet captions.** No "VIRAL" or "GAME-CHANGING" or "TRUST ME." The brand voice does the work.

## What we'd do differently

- The NYT Cooking paywall is the wrong economic model for Homemade's positioning ("free to use"). Homemade publishes the depth content for free and monetises later via marketplace, premium tier, and UGC commerce. The social strategy is therefore higher-funnel — pointing to free tutorials, building an email list, deferring monetisation.
- Visual identity is heavier. NYT Cooking pins are photo-led and house-style restrained but not particularly distinctive — they often blend into a Pinterest feed. Homemade's illustration-first identity should stand out more.
