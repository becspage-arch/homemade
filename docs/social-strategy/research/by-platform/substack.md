# Substack

## Why Substack matters for Homemade

Substack is the quiet giant of slow-living food and gardening media. The audience self-selects for long-form reading, the email format builds direct relationship without algorithmic mediation, and the voice closest to Homemade's reference set (Alice Waters, Nigel Slater, Monty Don, Erin Boyle) actually exists on Substack already.

The catch: Substack is overwhelmingly personality-driven. Of the top 25 food Substacks, 23 are named-author publications and only Vittles operates as a brand-channel magazine. The same pattern holds in gardening and crafts. Brand-channel Substacks are rare and tend to underperform the writers.

This shapes the strategy. Homemade can win on Substack only if it accepts that the publication needs a clear voice (even if not a clear face) that the audience can attach to. This is doable inside the existing brand voice constraints: the byline can be "Homemade" or an editorial voice rather than a personal name.

## What's working

### 1. The single-recipe-per-week format

The cleanest replicable model in food Substack.

**Caroline Chambers, What to Cook** (tens of thousands of subscribers). One complete-meal recipe delivered to inbox every Saturday morning. The format is the product. Subscribers pay to outsource one cooking decision per week.

**Alison Roman, A Newsletter** (300K+ free subscribers). Recipes, narrative, opinion. Personality-led at scale, but the format under the personality is replicable: a recipe, a story around it, a strong opening line.

**Victoria Minell** — 120K subscribers. Healthy twists on popular dishes. Single-recipe-per-week.

### 2. The food-magazine brand channel

**Vittles**: UK / India online food magazine. Long-form essays, recipes, commissioned writing. The brand carries the publication; individual writers contribute under it. This is the structural template for Homemade on Substack: the rare proven example of a non-personality food Substack at scale.

### 3. The slow-living crossover

**Sattva** (Nandita Iyer) — fortnightly newsletter spanning slow living, food, productivity, mindfulness, zero waste, gardening. Aimed at the exact audience Homemade serves. The byline is a named author but the content is genuinely cross-category, demonstrating that the audience is hungry for one trusted place rather than five specialists.

### 4. The single-author gardening newsletter

**The Gardening Mind** (Jo Thompson) — tens of thousands of subscribers in 165 countries. Garden-design content with a small-garden-design course attached. Membership chat thread for plant ID and problem-solving. The pattern: free newsletter is the front door, paid course or membership monetises.

**Wild Way** (Jack Wallington) — thousands of subscribers. Advanced gardening, wildflower communities for wildlife, edible cultivation. Yorkshire-based, lyrical writing in the Monty Don / Vita Sackville-West register. Single closest voice analogue to where Homemade's gardening content should sit.

### 5. The slow-craft newsletter

**Knit Together** (Anne Vally) — thousands of subscribers. Weekly letter encouraging readers to slow down and knit. "Pocket of wellness, joy, and inclusive knitting."

**Cherry Heart** (Sandra Paul) — thousands of subscribers. Quiet crochet, knitting, sewing.

**In the Wool** — twice-monthly knitting and crochet newsletter, yarn-led with patterns and discounts.

**Soft Crafts** (Ella Emhoff) — knitting, crochet, embroidery, upcycling.

These are all small relative to the food category. The crafts newsletter audience on Substack is engaged but the absolute numbers are five-figure ceilings for now.

## Format mix that works

- **The free weekly letter** is the front door. One specific recipe, garden task, or technique tutorial per week. 800–1,500 words. Opens with the content, not a personal intro.
- **A paid tier** can layer on top once the free list is engaged — paid threads, the deeper archive, a quarterly long essay. Homemade can defer this until well into Phase 8 since the free tutorials on homemade.education are already the depth play.
- **Recommendations and cross-posts** drive a meaningful portion of Substack growth. The platform's "recommends" mechanism is the closest thing Substack has to algorithmic discovery. Cross-recommendation with adjacent slow-living newsletters is the cheapest growth move on the platform.

## Hook patterns

Substack subject lines that work in the slow-living / food / garden space:

- Specific and small. "Sourdough that proves in the fridge overnight" beats "The ultimate guide to sourdough."
- Seasonal anchor. "Tomatoes this week" / "When to sow the chilli seeds."
- No clickbait, no urgency manipulation. The Substack audience is allergic.

The first line of the letter matters more than the subject. Open with a concrete observation, ingredient, or instruction.

## Cadence

Weekly is the strongest cadence in food and gardening Substacks. Fortnightly works for crafts. More frequent posts cannibalise open rates without a paid tier to support them.

A single weekly letter could be the spine of Homemade's Substack presence. Each letter is one tutorial from homemade.education translated to the email-reading register, with the brand voice intact. The CTA at the bottom is always "read the full tutorial on homemade.education."

## What Homemade can do that personality writers can't

A specific advantage of the brand-channel format on Substack: **breadth without identity drift**. Caroline Chambers can't credibly write about both sourdough technique and ornamental gardening — her audience expects her food point of view. Homemade can, because the voice is editorial rather than personal. One week tomatoes, the next sourdough, the next a granny square. The audience comes for the trusted source, not the trusted person.

## Verdict for Homemade

Plausible fit, with one constraint to manage: the voice has to be tight enough that the audience attaches to "Homemade" the way they attach to a writer. This is doable because the voice rules are already locked. Substack is not the lead platform but it's the strongest medium-term play for direct audience relationship — and the day the splash gate drops, the first 500 subscribers can be acquired from cross-recommendations with adjacent newsletters.
