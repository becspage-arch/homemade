# Instagram

## Why Instagram is the most cautious platform in this set

Instagram remains the photography-led discovery surface for the slow-living audience, particularly in cooking, baking, gardening and crochet. Crucially for Homemade, Instagram does not require a face — many of the largest accounts in cooking and baking are run as brand channels or are functionally faceless even when a named creator owns the bio.

The caution flags are:

- **Bot detection.** Instagram is the most aggressive of the platforms about detecting automation. We're not invoking Chrome MCP in this session, and any future research should also avoid logged-in browsing of competitor accounts.
- **Hashtags are increasingly cosmetic.** What was a 30-tag wall in 2019 is now a 3–5 tag light touch with most reach driven by Reels and the Explore page.
- **Reels dominates reach.** A static photo post in 2026 reaches a fraction of the followers it did three years ago. The brand presence works only when Reels are part of the mix.

## What's working for non-personality-led content

### 1. Sourdough and bread brand-channel accounts

Bread Instagram is the strongest non-personality-led niche on the platform. The dough is the protagonist; the baker's face appears rarely if at all.

- `@shebakesourdough` (Jade Lim) — ~2M followers. Hands-only and finished-loaf photography. The author exists but the camera is on the dough.
- `@amybakesbread` (Amy Coyne) — ~858K followers. Sourdough recipes and help, photography-led.
- `@everything.sourdough` (Deb Lemos) — ~381K followers. Teaching-led, hands and loaves.
- `@sourdough_explained` (Andra Stefanescu) — ~379K followers. Process explanations, hands and dough.
- `@breadbosses` — community brand-channel account.
- `@brooklynsourdough` — bakery brand channel.

These accounts are templates Homemade can study directly. Mix Reels (the kneading, scoring and baking process), carousel posts (a recipe in 8 frames), and the occasional static finished-loaf shot.

### 2. Pattern-house knitting accounts

The Scandi pattern-house format is the working analogue for a brand-channel knitting presence. The garment is on a body, the photography is editorial, the designer's name is on the bio but they're rarely the subject of the shot.

- `@petiteknit` (Mette Wendelboe Okkels) — ~1.3M followers. Danish pattern house, brand-channel format dominant. Reels of garments being worn, occasional close-up of stitches and yarn. Personal posts are a minority.
- `@brooklyntweed` (Jared Flood) — ~175K followers. American yarn and pattern brand. Hands, yarn, swatches, garments on bodies. Pure brand channel.

### 3. Recipe / how-to publisher brand channels

The traditional brand-channel publishers run Instagram presences that mirror their Pinterest strategy: high-production photography or video, brand voice in the captions, recipe link in bio.

- `@nytcooking` — ~2.8M followers as of 2021, likely substantially larger now. Reels-led, no presenter.
- `@bbcgoodfood` — ~1.4M followers. Brand-channel format, mixed Reels and static.
- `@kingarthurbaking` — recipe brand channel, large presence.
- `@food52` — recipe brand channel, mixed photography and Reels.

These are the structural templates for Homemade on Instagram.

### 4. Gardening on Instagram (the cautious bit)

UK gardening Instagram is heavily personality-led — Monty Don, Adam Frost, Frances Tophill, Alan Titchmarsh, Ramona Jones. Even the landscape-design accounts (Tom Stuart-Smith, Charlie Harpur) lean on the named designer. Brand-channel accounts in this space (RHS, BBC Gardeners' World magazine) exist but the engagement-per-follower is meaningfully lower than the personality accounts.

The gardening-Instagram play for a brand channel is therefore narrower: heavy on illustration, heavy on garden-tour b-roll without a presenter, heavy on educational carousel content (companion planting charts, frost-date calendars, sowing schedules). Less feed-presence, more saves-and-shares.

## Format mix that works

- **Reels:** the primary growth surface. 60–90 seconds, vertical, overhead and hands-only. The same source video that powers TikTok cross-posts here with minimal re-editing.
- **Carousels:** 5–10 photos or illustrated frames. Higher save and share rates than single posts. Strong for technique tutorials and ingredient/variety guides. The Homemade illustration style suits carousels especially well.
- **Static photo posts:** lower reach now but still useful for finished-result shots that anchor the brand aesthetic. Don't lead with these.
- **Stories:** lower-priority. Useful for behind-the-tutorial work once an audience exists; not a discovery surface for new followers.

## Caption patterns

Instagram captions can run longer than TikTok and the audience reads them. A working pattern:

- First line: a hook sentence that reads well in the truncated preview.
- A short paragraph of context (1–2 sentences).
- The headline recipe or instruction.
- A specific detail or substitution note.
- A quiet CTA: "full tutorial on homemade.education" or "saved for the weekend."

No emoji clusters at the top. No emoji-spaced lines. The brand voice in captions matters more than on TikTok because the audience reads them more carefully.

## Hashtag patterns

3–5 tags maximum. The same broad / mid-niche / tight / brand structure as TikTok. Hashtags drive negligible reach now but don't hurt.

## Cadence

3–5 Reels per week is the sustainable working cadence. Plus 1–2 carousels and the odd static. Instagram penalises long gaps less than TikTok does, so the platform tolerates a slower start while the team finds its rhythm.

## Verdict for Homemade

Plausible fit but not the lead platform. Instagram is the secondary surface that catches the engaged audience who follow the brand, but the discovery economics of cold reach favour TikTok (cooking, baking) and Pinterest (gardening, crafts, depth content). Instagram is where Homemade earns trust with the already-aware reader, not where it acquires the audience initially.
