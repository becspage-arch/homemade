# Synthesis

The compressed read on the research. Patterns Homemade can act on.

## Five cross-niche patterns

### 1. Overhead-and-hands-only is the universal video grammar

Every niche we examined (cooking, baking, gardening, crochet, knitting) has a working faceless format built around a fixed overhead camera and hands entering frame. The format crosses cuisines (Japanese, Korean, French bakery), crosses crafts (sourdough, granny squares, garden beds), and crosses platforms (TikTok, Reels, Shorts, YouTube long-form). It is the safest single format bet Homemade can make.

The format scales from 30-second TikToks (Le Marais Bakery, ikneadbread) to 15-minute YouTube videos (Peaceful Cuisine, Cooking Tree). The same camera setup, same lighting, same prop set produces content for every duration.

### 2. The strongest faceless brands replace the personality with a consistent kitchen or garden

Peaceful Cuisine's warm-wood Japanese kitchen, Cooking Tree's white Korean café aesthetic, Le Marais Bakery's stainless-steel production line, GrowVeg's illustrated diagrams, PetiteKnit's Scandi daylight rooms — each one carries the brand identity in the staging rather than in a presenter. The audience recognises the channel by the worktop, the props, the light, the typography on screen. This is achievable for Homemade from the first post because the brand direction is fully locked.

### 3. Saves beat likes for replicable craft content

Across all five niches, the content that compounds is the content that earns saves and bookmarks — troubleshooting carousels, scoring patterns, sowing schedules, stitch dictionaries, technique posts. Likes are vanity; saves predict watch-completion, return visits, and link clicks. Designing for the save means treating each post as a reference the audience will come back to.

Homemade's already-shipped bookmark feature is the structural fit for this audience behaviour.

### 4. Sound design out-performs music selection

Recipe demos with ambient cooking sound (chopping, sizzling, pouring) consistently out-engage the same recipe with music. This holds on TikTok, Reels and YouTube. The same observation applies to gardening (trowel in soil, secateurs cutting, hands brushing leaves) and to crafts (yarn against needles, scissors snipping, paper unwrapping a pattern).

A clip-on microphone is the single cheapest production upgrade. It outperforms any visual investment for the audio-on majority of viewers.

### 5. The publishing-page-then-pin loop is the highest-ROI cross-platform pattern

The accounts that compound traffic over years (Food52, BBC Good Food, NYT Cooking, King Arthur Baking, Lion Brand) all run a tutorial-then-Pinterest pipeline: each piece of long-form content on the website produces 3–10 pins, each pin earns multi-year impressions. Pinterest is the single platform where evergreen content outperforms recent content, so volume builds permanent traffic.

Homemade can do this from launch — the tutorial library is the source material; pin generation can be added to the publishing pipeline.

## Three niche-specific patterns

### Cooking and baking: ASMR is platform-native

The aesthetic-meditative format (Peaceful Cuisine, Cooking Tree) and the ASMR-led format (Sam Seats, ikneadbread) overlap. Both can carry a faceless cooking channel from zero to seven figures of followers. No other niche has this dual-format runway.

### Gardening: seasonality writes the calendar

Sowing dates, harvest windows, frost timings and bloom times structure the year. Successful gardening accounts (vegetablegarden02, Marf's Kitchen Garden, GrowVeg) plan content months ahead because the seasons dictate when to post. This is the friendliest niche for a planning-led content calendar.

### Crochet and knitting: pattern depth is the engagement engine

Pattern reference content earns saves and pins for years. The successful brand-channel textile accounts (PetiteKnit, Brooklyn Tweed, Lalylaland) treat each pattern release as a launch event. Homemade's pattern library is the structural asset.

## Platform-by-platform: what works without a personality

### TikTok

Overhead recipe / craft demos with hands-only framing. Calm music or pure ambient sound. Text overlays in serif type carry the instructions. Three to five posts per week sustainable cadence. Captions short. Hashtags three to five tags. The platform with the strongest discovery for new brand-channel accounts.

### YouTube long-form

The meditative overhead at 8–15 minutes (Peaceful Cuisine / Cooking Tree model) is the closest format match to Homemade's brand register in the entire research set. Weekly cadence. Shorts as cross-cuts from the long-form. Chapter timestamps materially increase watch time and ranking.

### Pinterest

The highest-leverage platform for evergreen traffic. Single dominant photograph or illustration, serif title overlay, link to tutorial page. Boards as long-term assets. Five to fifteen pins per day from existing tutorial content is sustainable. The audience demographic is the closest match to Homemade's stated audience.

### Instagram

Reels are the discovery surface; carousels are the high-saves educational surface; static posts are the brand-anchor. 3–5 Reels per week with 1–2 carousels. Best fit is the brand-channel publisher pattern (BBC Good Food, King Arthur Baking, NYT Cooking) plus the sub-niche brand-channel formats (sourdough Instagram, knitting pattern houses). UK gardening Instagram is personality-saturated and a harder entry.

### Substack

Personality-dominated. The brand-channel exceptions (Vittles, mid-scale gardening newsletters like The Gardening Mind, Wild Way) demonstrate that an editorial voice can succeed without a named author face if the writing register is tight. Weekly cadence in food and gardening; fortnightly in crafts. Cross-recommendations are the primary growth mechanism.

## Cross-posting pollination patterns

The most efficient flow for a small team:

1. **Anchor: a single long-form YouTube video.** 8–15 minutes, calm overhead, brand-staged kitchen / garden / craft worktop.
2. **Cut 1: Two to four YouTube Shorts.** 30–60 seconds each, taken from the most visually rich moments of the long-form.
3. **Cut 2: The same Shorts (or close variants) posted as TikToks.** Different captions; otherwise identical.
4. **Cut 3: The same Shorts posted as Instagram Reels.** Different captions again; otherwise identical.
5. **Pin set: 5–10 Pinterest pins.** Hero illustration pin, supplies pin, step pins, finished-result pin, glossary or chart pin where relevant. Generated from the published tutorial page.
6. **Newsletter: a single Substack letter** referencing the published tutorial and adding the editorial framing — a paragraph or two of context, the link, a brief secondary note.
7. **Carousel post on Instagram** built from the tutorial's TipTap structure, with 8–10 illustrated frames covering the technique.

Production input: one tutorial + one shooting day + one editing pass. Output: 1 long-form, 2–4 Shorts, 2–4 TikToks, 2–4 Reels, 5–10 pins, 1 newsletter, 1 carousel. That's the multiplier the cross-posting pattern unlocks.

## Hook patterns that recur

- **Lead with the finished thing.** The first frame is the loaf, the harvest basket, the finished granny square. Then cut to the start.
- **Lead with the most satisfying sound.** A dough slap, a knife crunch, a pouring jug — used in the first 1–3 seconds as the audio hook.
- **Lead with the specific surprising detail.** "Sourdough that proves overnight in the fridge." "Tomatoes that ripen by mid-July." "A granny square in a single colour."
- **Do not lead with a verbal hook.** "Stop scrolling," "Wait for it," "You won't believe" — these are wrong for the Homemade brand voice and are increasingly punished by audience scrolling behaviour anyway.

## Caption and description patterns

- **Short and concrete.** First line is the recipe or technique name. Second line is one specific detail or substitution. Third line, if any, is a quiet CTA pointing to the full tutorial.
- **British English.** All measurements metric-first.
- **No banned phrases.** The voice-rule list applies to social captions as much as to tutorial body copy.
- **No wrap-up sentence.** Just stop.

## What the field is not doing that Homemade can

1. **An English-rooted, brand-channel version of the Peaceful Cuisine / Cooking Tree format.** The category is established globally but the English-language slow-living version does not exist.
2. **A brand-channel UK gardening publication with the visual standard of the personality accounts.** The lane is open between RHS-style institutional content and Monty-Don-style presenter-led content.
3. **An editorial-voice food / craft / garden Substack that doesn't depend on a single name.** Vittles is the only proven exception in food; the crafts and gardening Substack space has no clear brand-channel equivalent.
4. **Illustration-first social content in cooking and gardening.** Almost every competitor is photo-led; the Homemade illustration style would be visibly distinctive on any feed.

## What the research couldn't answer

- Exact growth velocity for shortlisted accounts in the last 90 days. Social Blade was inaccessible from the WebFetch tool in this session.
- Engagement-rate benchmarks (likes : followers, saves : impressions). Most marketing aggregators report follower counts not engagement.
- Comparative performance of AI-narrated stop-motion in any niche. The format is recent enough that there's no documented exemplar at scale yet.
- The economics of the niche Substacks (paid vs. free split, paid-tier price points). Substack profiles do not display paid-tier subscriber counts.

These gaps are flagged so the playbook treats its claims as hypotheses to test against real posting data, not as predictions.
