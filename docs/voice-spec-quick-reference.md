# Voice spec — quick reference (MANDATORY pre-read)

**Status:** Canonical. Read this end-to-end before drafting any
tutorial. Apply the worked examples and the self-critique below to
every opening paragraph. This is the single source of truth that the
autopilot, the qc-fix routine, and every author prompt point at.

This file distils `docs/voice-spec-2026-05-21.md` into the parts you
actually use while drafting. Read the full spec when you need the
research and the why; read this one before you write.

---

## 1. The Mary Berry test

The bar is Mary Berry / Martha Stewart, not academic Wikipedia and not
poetic essayist. A real homemaker writes the tutorial after her tenth
batch of the thing. Concrete tests on any opening paragraph:

1. Could a 12-year-old read it aloud and roughly understand?
2. Does it sound like a knowledgeable friend at the kitchen table?
3. Could a stranger skim it in 30 seconds and know what she's about to
   do, what for, and (if cooking) what comes out?
4. Are there zero academic citations, institutional names, Latin
   binomials, or clinical jargon in the body?
5. Are sentences mostly 8–15 words?

If the answer to any test is no, the opening is wrong. Rewrite before
voice-check.

## 2. The locked opening pattern per content type

The first paragraph of every body follows the type's pattern. Three
short sentences is enough. An optional second short paragraph is
allowed only if it earns its place (a sensory note, a practical
warning).

**Recipe (cooking + baking, type=RECIPE).**
[What the dish is, in one short clause.] [What it gives you — texture,
flavour, what to serve it with.] [One sentence on what makes it work
or what to watch for.]

**Herbal remedy (type=REMEDY).**
[Name the preparation: what it is, what it's made of.] [State factually
what tradition has long used it for.] [What to expect at the end —
texture, taste, intrinsic yield, shelf life.]

**Herb profile (type=HERB_PROFILE).**
[Plant in the home kitchen — what the dried herb does, how it's
brewed, what to do with it.] [What tradition has used it for.] [The
jar / cupboard / shelf-life anchor.]

**Mindset practice (type=PRACTICE / READING).**
[Name the practice: what you'll do, in one short clause.] [State what
it's for — one sentence on the intended shift.] [What it'll feel like
or how it sits in the day.]

**Craft technique (type=TECHNIQUE).**
[Name the technique: what it is, when you use it.] [Optional: the
other names for it.] [What makes it work or what's hard about it.]

**Craft project (type=PATTERN).**
[Name the finished item: what it is, what it's for, who it fits / how
big it is.] [The technique base.] [Skill level + intrinsic yield.]

**Growing guide (type=GROWING_GUIDE).**
[Name the crop and the climate / zone assumption.] [The sowing-to-
harvest arc.] [The conditions it needs.]

**Home repair (type=HOME_REPAIR).**
[Name the repair in plain English.] [The approach.] [What you need.]

**Natural home recipe (type=RECIPE, category=natural-home).**
[Name the preparation: what it is, what's in it.] [What it's for and
how the result feels.] [Yield + cure time where it applies.]

## 3. Worked bad → good rewrites (apply these patterns by hand)

These are the calibration cases. Each shows a real bad opening
alongside its rewrite. Match the shape, not the exact words.

### Recipe (shortcrust)

> **Bad.** "There is something deeply satisfying about a properly made
> shortcrust pastry. In the world of baking, few skills are as
> foundational as the ability to bring together flour, fat, and water
> into a workable dough. The technique has been documented since Hannah
> Glasse's *The Art of Cookery Made Plain and Easy* (1747), and the
> French term *pâte brisée* reflects its place in the continental
> tradition."

> **Good.** "Shortcrust is the basic short pastry — flour, cold butter,
> a little water — for pies, tarts, and quiches. The job is to keep the
> butter cold and stop short of overworking the dough. Get those two
> right and the pastry comes out crisp and tender. The dough needs an
> hour in the fridge before rolling."

What changed: throat-clearing cut; "In the world of baking" cut;
Hannah Glasse + Latin to sourceNotes; concrete first sentence; what to
watch for; intrinsic process duration (the rest, not "your work
time").

### Herbal remedy (thyme cough syrup)

> **Bad.** "Thyme simmered in water, strained, cooled, and stirred into
> honey. This is the kitchen's oldest cough remedy, the combination of
> thyme's antispasmodic and expectorant action with honey's coating and
> preserving properties makes a syrup that soothes a dry, tickling
> cough and helps loosen stubborn mucus. Culpeper (1652) prescribed
> thyme for coughs and chest complaints; the German Commission E, which
> reviews traditional herbal medicines, confirms its traditional use
> for upper respiratory catarrh."

> **Good.** "Thyme syrup is a sweet, dark syrup of fresh thyme simmered
> in water and stirred into honey. A kitchen tradition long made for
> dry, tickly coughs and a sore chest. The thyme softens the cough; the
> honey coats the throat and keeps the syrup good in the fridge for
> about three weeks."

What changed: full first sentence with what the thing IS; "kitchen
tradition long made for" replaces "oldest cough remedy"; clinical
vocabulary swapped for plain English; Culpeper + Commission E to
sourceNotes; intrinsic shelf life as the anchor (not "40 minutes of
work").

### Herb profile (chamomile)

> **Bad.** "Chamomile is the western kitchen's end-of-day cup. It is
> recorded in almost every English-language home-medicine book of the
> last four centuries. The plant is a small annual with feathery leaves
> and white-petalled flowers with a yellow centre. The flowers carry
> the action. The kitchen meets them as dried whole flowers in the tea
> cup."

> **Good.** "Chamomile in the home kitchen, what the dried flowers do,
> how to brew them properly, and what to do with them beyond the
> evening cup. The most-used herb of the European home cupboard, long
> taken for a wound-up day, a cramping gut, or a sore eye. A jar of
> dried flowers keeps a year in the kitchen cupboard and makes about
> thirty cups of strong brew."

What changed: the botanical lecture ("The plant is a small annual with
feathery leaves...") cut; the metaphorical first sentence ("western
kitchen's end-of-day cup") replaced with a working description; "long
taken for" rather than "recorded in books"; intrinsic shelf life +
yield, no time-of-work.

### Mindset practice

> **Bad.** "Take a deep breath and let yourself arrive in this moment.
> Honour the wisdom of your body as you settle into this practice.
> Notice whatever arises — a sensation, a thought, an old story — and
> let it move through you with compassion. There is no right way to do
> this work; trust that your nervous system knows what it needs."

> **Good.** "Sit somewhere quiet. Read each line below out loud, once.
> Pause for two breaths between lines. If a line lands as true, stay
> with it for an extra breath. If it doesn't, move on."

What changed: "arrive in this moment" cut; "honour the wisdom of your
body" cut; "whatever arises" cut; instruction-led, decision-rule, no
ceremony language.

### Craft technique (magic ring)

> **Bad.** "The magic ring, also known as the adjustable starting loop
> or magic circle, is a foundational technique in modern crochet that
> allows the crafter to begin a project worked in the round without
> leaving a hole at the centre. The technique was popularised in the
> mid-twentieth century..."

> **Good.** "Magic ring is the adjustable starting loop for crochet
> worked in the round. You start by wrapping the yarn around two
> fingers, draw up a loop, work the first round of stitches into the
> loop, then pull the tail to close the hole at the centre. The only
> tricky bit is keeping hold of the tail while you work the first
> round, otherwise the loop can come undone."

What changed: "foundational technique in modern crochet" cut; date
moved to sourceNotes; restated in plain English; what's actually hard
about it.

### Growing guide (tomato)

> **Bad.** "Solanum lycopersicum, the cultivated tomato, is one of the
> most beloved horticultural subjects in the temperate garden. RHS
> guidance categorises it as a tender annual requiring 18–24°C minimum
> night temperatures for fruit set..."

> **Good.** "Tomatoes are a warm-summer crop. In the UK you sow indoors
> in March or April, plant out after the last frost in late May, and
> pick from July through October. They need full sun, a deep pot or
> rich border soil, and steady watering once they set fruit. Cordon
> varieties grow tall and need a stake; bush varieties stay low and
> don't."

What changed: Latin binomial cut; RHS to sourceNotes; concrete arc;
plain-English variety distinction.

### Home repair (plasterboard)

> **Bad.** "Resolving a perforation in plasterboard is a common
> domestic maintenance task that any competent householder should be
> capable of executing. Before commencing any cutting or fastening
> operations, ensure you have appropriate personal protective
> equipment..."

> **Good.** "A plasterboard hole — from a doorknob, a falling shelf
> bracket, a kicked skirting — fixes with a square patch glued in
> flush with the wall, filler skimmed over the joint, sanded smooth
> when dry. Most repairs use offcuts from a builder's merchant; tools
> are a craft knife, a filler knife, and a sanding block."

What changed: "Resolving a perforation" → "A plasterboard hole";
"competent householder" cut; PPE paragraph cut; closing line names
the kit.

### Natural home (oatmeal soap)

> **Bad.** "This cold-process oatmeal soap recipe represents the
> traditional saponification of olive, coconut, and castor oils through
> the careful incorporation of a sodium hydroxide solution into the
> heated fat matrix..."

> **Good.** "Oatmeal soap is a mild, creamy bar made from olive,
> coconut, and castor oils, with porridge oats stirred through for a
> soft exfoliating finish. It lathers well and is kind on dry or
> sensitive skin. Makes about eight standard bars; the bars cure for
> 4–6 weeks before they're ready to use."

What changed: "saponification" cut; "sodium hydroxide" appears as
"lye" in the steps where it matters, not in the orientation; intrinsic
yield + cure time, no time-of-work.

## 4. Hard "don't"s

- **No subjective time-of-work claims.** No "Twelve minutes' work",
  "Twenty-five minutes' work", "About thirty minutes of active work",
  "spread over an afternoon". The Tutorial schema carries timeMinutes /
  prepMinutes / cookMinutes / yieldDescription / servings — those
  render in the info bar. Subjective time-of-work is platitude
  territory and assumes an uninterrupted kitchen with no children,
  no disability, no pressure. Drop it everywhere.
  - **OK:** intrinsic process duration ("steeped overnight",
    "simmered for thirty minutes", "cured for four to six weeks"),
    intrinsic yield ("makes about 350 ml of syrup, enough for
    thirty-five doses"), shelf life ("keeps a year in a cool
    cupboard").
  - **Not OK:** anything that estimates the cook's effort time in
    abstract minutes.

- **No academic citations in body prose.** Years in parentheses
  ("(1652)"), institutional names (German Commission E, BHP, FDA,
  MHRA, NHS, RHS, USDA, ESCOP, EMA), historical figures without a
  plain-English gloss (Culpeper, Beeton, de Dillmont, Caulfeild,
  Grieve, Gerard, Quincy, Glasse, Acton, Jekyll, Sackville-West).
  All go to `sourceNotes`.

- **No clinical / Latin vocabulary in body prose.** catarrh,
  expectorant, antispasmodic, demulcent, anodyne, carminative,
  vulnerary, sedative-action, volatile-oils, mucilage, tincture,
  decoction, infusion, maceration, monograph, vermifuge,
  diaphoretic, alterative, nervine, adaptogen, constituents,
  saponification, anhydrous, determinate, indeterminate, materia-
  medica, western herbal canon, single-herb profile, most-cited,
  contraindication, phytochemical, pharmacology, efficacy.
  Use plain English or a glossaryTooltip with a registered slug.

- **No "the plant is a small annual with feathery leaves" botanical
  lecture in the first paragraph.** Plant morphology, family
  taxonomy, "native to X", height ranges all go to a "Botanical" /
  "Technical notes" section below the orientation, or to sourceNotes.

- **No soft medical / efficacy claims** in herbal medicine: "safe to
  take", "fine for almost everyone", "well-tolerated", "perfect for
  X cold", "ideal for sleep", "good for digestion", "cures X",
  "guaranteed to work". Locked medical disclaimer phrasing only,
  once, in an infoPanel at the foot of the body.

- **No prose-style sequential steps.** "Steep 8–10 minutes. Strain.
  Apply." is an `orderedList` written badly.

- **No wrap-up close.** No "Happy baking", no "Remember that ...",
  no philosophical sign-off. The last useful sentence is the last
  sentence.

## 5. Self-critique (do this BEFORE running voice-check)

Read the opening paragraph aloud. Walk these in order. If any answer
is no, rewrite and re-read.

1. Does the first sentence carry the secret in plain English? (Not a
   metaphor, not an academic citation, not "There is something
   satisfying about".)
2. Does the second sentence say what it's for, in plain English?
3. Does the third sentence give an intrinsic anchor — yield, shelf
   life, process duration — without making a subjective time-of-work
   claim?
4. Is there zero clinical / Latin vocabulary in the body without a
   glossaryTooltip wrap?
5. Is there zero botanical morphology in the first paragraph?
6. Are years, institutional names, and historical figures all in
   sourceNotes, not the body?
7. Are sequential steps in an `orderedList`, not in prose?
8. Are sentences mostly 8–15 words?
9. Would a 12-year-old read it aloud and roughly understand?
10. Does it sound like Mary Berry / Martha Stewart, not Wikipedia and
    not a model trying to look credible?

A passing brief answers yes to all ten.

## 6. When in doubt

Read the worked rewrites in §3 again. Pick the one that most matches
your content type. Match the shape, not the exact words. If your draft
opening doesn't read like the "Good" example, it's not done yet.
